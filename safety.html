Safety: The body's prerequisite for intimacy

Safety is not a feeling you have. It is a structure you build. This is what that means.

By Juliette Karaman · Trauma-Informed Intimacy Expert

Most conversations about intimacy start in the wrong place. They start with desire, or connection, or communication, all of which matter enormously, but they skip the step that has to come first.

The body has to feel safe.

Not comfortable. Not happy. Not even in love. Safe. The nervous system's basic assessment (is there threat here?) has to come back negative before the body will open to anything.

What Safety Actually Is

Safety is not a feeling you generate through positive thinking. It is not something you talk yourself into. It is a physiological state, a signal from the nervous system that the environment has been assessed and found to not require a threat response.

The body is running this assessment constantly. It is reading the room: the tone of a voice, the tension in a body, the quality of someone's attention, whether the approach felt like reaching toward or reaching for. These signals arrive below conscious thought and are processed before the thinking mind has any input.

This is why you can be with someone who says all the right things and still feel your body closing, and why you can be with someone in complete silence and feel completely open. The words are not the data. The body is reading something else entirely.

"Safety is a structure, not a feeling. You do not wait for it to arrive. You build the conditions in which the nervous system can produce it."

Why Safety Disappears in Long Relationships

This is the part that surprises people most: safety can erode in relationships where love is completely intact. The love hasn't gone. The nervous system has simply accumulated data.

Every moment of tension that was not fully resolved leaves a residue. Every time touch was offered and received as pressure rather than presence. Every argument that ended without real repair. The body remembers all of it, not as memory, as patterned response. A slight tightening when he comes to bed. A breath that goes shallow when she reaches over. Not a decision. A learned prediction.

The nervous system is doing its job: protecting. What it needs is new data. Consistent, repeated, specific data that says: here is safe. Here you can relax.

How to Build Safety Deliberately

Consistency over intensity

The nervous system trusts what is reliable. One extraordinary evening does not overwrite years of low-grade tension. Fifteen minutes, every day, of something the body can count on: that accumulates. Small and consistent beats large and occasional every time.

Specificity over generality

Vague intention keeps the nervous system alert. Specific agreements, we are doing this, for this long, and then it ends, give the body something concrete to relax into. See structured touch → for how this works in practice.

Anchoring in the present

The threat response lives in anticipation and memory. The present moment, when it can be accessed, is almost always safe. Touch Base® is the practice that brings the nervous system back to what is actually happening right now, which is, usually, fine.

Repair after rupture

No relationship avoids rupture. The question is what follows it. Complete, specific repair restores safety. A quick "sorry" is not it. "I see what happened and I understand how that landed" is. Partial repair or avoided repair leaves residue. The body keeps the score.

Safety and Sexual Trauma

For people who carry sexual trauma, the safety question is more complex, and more urgent. The nervous system of someone who has experienced sexual trauma has very good reasons for its vigilance. The protective response that makes intimacy difficult is not a malfunction. It is a completely accurate response to a real past threat.

The work of rebuilding safety after trauma is not about overriding that response. It is about slowly, patiently, specifically, giving the nervous system new data. Showing it, through repeated experience, not through argument, that here, now, with this person, in this structure, the protection is no longer needed.

This is the core of Juliette's trauma-informed approach. Not pushing through. Not performing wellness. Building the conditions in which the body's own wisdom can, finally, let go.
