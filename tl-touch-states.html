The Touch States: where the body is right now

Not where you want it to be. Not where it used to be. Where it actually is, in this moment, in relation to touch.

By Juliette Karaman · Trauma-Informed Intimacy Expert

The Touch States are one of four frameworks in Juliette Karaman's body of work. They are distinct from the Touch Languages™ (how the body gives and receives touch), the Touch Patterns™ (what the body learned to guard with), and the Stages of Touch (how far relationship disconnection has travelled over time).

Where those frameworks describe the shape of a person or a relationship, the Touch States describe the weather. They are the body's current position, not its history, not its character, not its relationship arc. Where it is right now, today, in this moment.

This distinction matters enormously in practice. A person can be primarily a Holder pattern, have an Emotional Touch Language, be in Stage 2 of relationship disconnection, and still be in a different Touch State on a Tuesday evening than they are on a Saturday morning. The state is not fixed. It shifts. Knowing which state you and your partner are in before any intimate contact is the difference between reaching each other and missing entirely.

The Five Touch States

Braced

The body is actively protecting. The jaw sets before the hand arrives. The back tightens. Some part of the nervous system has already decided, before any touch has occurred, sometimes before the partner has moved, that what is coming requires defending against.

Braced is not a choice. It is a prediction the body makes, based on everything it has learned about what touch in this context tends to mean. The person in it often knows they are braced and hates it. They may love their partner completely. The body is operating on older data.

From Braced, the first move is never toward another person. It is toward the body's own safety. Touch Base® is the starting point. Solo. Then, once the nervous system has found the present moment, the question of partner contact becomes possible again.

Withheld

There is sensation available but it is being held back. The body is present but not allowing itself to be fully felt or fully expressed. Often experienced as a kind of flatness, or the sense of observing from a slight remove. Being there without being in it.

Withheld is usually protective. The body learned at some point that opening fully was not safe, or was not received, or led somewhere difficult. It has found a middle position: present enough to maintain connection, closed enough to stay safe.

From Withheld, the work is gentle invitation, not escalation. Pushing for more expression drives the state deeper. What moves it is the consistent experience of safety, that opening a little is received without demand for more.

Performing

The body is going through the motions. There may be responsiveness, even apparent enthusiasm, but it is monitored, produced for the partner's response rather than genuinely felt. The person is not in their experience. They are managing someone else's experience of them.

Performing is exhausting in a way that is invisible from outside. The partner sees engagement. The person feels the labour of maintaining it. This is the most dangerous state in long-term relationships. It is the state most likely to be invisible to both people for years, until one day the person realises they cannot remember the last time they were actually in their own body during intimacy.

From Performing, the work is finding one real thing. One moment of genuine sensation. One response that was not produced for an audience. Starting there, however small, rather than trying to stop the performance wholesale.

Present

The body is here. There is genuine sensation, genuine contact, genuine response. Not perfect. Not necessarily free of all protection. Actually in the room, receiving what is happening, responding from what is actually felt.

Present is not a peak state. It is the baseline from which intimacy becomes possible. It is also, for many people in long relationships, a state they have not consistently inhabited in years. The return to Present, not to peak experience, just to genuine presence, is often described by clients as more moving than anything more elaborate they had expected to be working toward.

From Present, the work is staying. Noticing when the mind pulls away, into performance, into monitoring, into the past or future, and returning. Building the capacity to remain rather than the drive to escalate.

Melted

Full reception. The body has released the protection it usually carries and is genuinely open to what is arriving. This is not passive. It requires a quality of active surrender that is only possible when significant accumulated safety is already in place.

Melted is not a permanent state. It is not a goal to be achieved and maintained. It is what becomes available, sometimes, when enough consistent safety has been built that the body decides, below conscious thought, that the protection is no longer needed here.

Melted cannot be forced, performed, or willed into existence. It is the nervous system's gift to itself when the conditions have been sufficiently and repeatedly met. This is why structure, not spontaneity, not intensity, not romantic gesture, is what creates the conditions for it.

How the Touch States Differ from the Stages of Touch

This is the distinction most people need made explicit:

The Touch States are moment-to-moment. They describe where the body is right now, this evening, in this context. They can shift within a single conversation. A person can move from Braced to Present within a session if the conditions change.

The Stages of Touch, The Quiet, The Brace, The Gone, describe the arc of a relationship's disconnection over time. They are not about where the body is in this moment. They are about how far the distance has travelled and how long it has been accumulating. They change over months, not minutes.

A couple can be in Stage 2 (The Brace, where disconnection has become the pattern) and still, in a given evening, move through all five Touch States depending on what happens between them. The Stage describes the territory. The State describes where you are standing in it right now.

"The Touch State is the weather. The Stage of Touch is the climate. You can have a warm day in winter. You can have a cold front in summer. Knowing which you are dealing with changes what you do about it."

Using the Touch States in Practice

The single most useful practice Juliette teaches around the Touch States is the check-in before contact. Before any intimate touch, before a structured touch date, before any moment of reaching for a partner, each person names their current state.

Not as a report. Not as an invitation to fix. Just: here is where I am.

This changes everything about what happens next. When both people know where they are starting from, the reaching adjusts automatically. A partner who knows their person is in Withheld does not escalate. A person who names themselves as Braced has already begun moving out of it. The naming alone creates a small shift, because it brings the body into the present moment of observation rather than the automatic running of the protective pattern.

The Touch States ladder, Braced → Withheld → Performing → Present → Melted, is also the map for the Touch Reset Quiz. It identifies your current state and gives you a starting point that matches where you actually are, rather than where you think you should be.
