Common Questions · Juliette Karaman

Why did we stop touching?

The most common question in couples work. The answer is not what most people think.

By Juliette Karaman · Trauma-Informed Intimacy Expert

It is the question that arrives in different forms, but it is always the same question. Why did we stop? When did it happen? Why can't we get back?

Sometimes there is a moment people point to: a pregnancy, a loss, a particular argument. More often there is no moment. There is just the gradual, quiet accumulation of a thousand small things, until one day both people realise that a peck on the cheek is all that happens, and neither person reaches first anymore.

The answer is almost never that the love has gone. The answer is almost always that the body has learned to protect itself.

How the Body Learns to Brace

The nervous system is a learning machine. Every experience of touch that did not land the way it was intended, every moment when one person reached and the other was not quite there, every time touch came with an unspoken agenda, every attempt at intimacy that ended in tension or silence, leaves a trace.

The body is not dramatic about this. It does not record it as a wound. It simply updates its prediction: when touch comes in this context, here is what to expect. It adjusts accordingly, tightening slightly, going a little further away, becoming a little less available.

Over years, these micro-adjustments accumulate into a pattern. The body that once opened freely now needs something different to open at all. Not because it is shut down. Because it learned.

The Most Common Patterns

Touch that came with an agenda

When touch reliably leads somewhere specific, to sex, to a particular response, to reassurance, the body learns to associate touch with pressure. Not danger. Pressure, and pressure, over time, produces the same response as a mild threat: bracing, withdrawal, going somewhere else mentally. The body is not saying no to the person. It is saying no to what it has learned touch means.

The pursuer and the withdrawer

One person reaches. The other retreats. The reaching intensifies. The retreat deepens. This is one of the most well-documented patterns in relationship research, and it is a nervous system pattern, not a character flaw. The pursuer is trying to re-establish connection through proximity. The withdrawer is trying to regulate by creating space. Both are doing exactly what their nervous system learned to do. Neither is winning.

Touch lost in translation

Two people can want the same thing, closeness, presence, being met, and still miss each other completely, because they are offering it in entirely different forms. One reaches physically. The other needs words first. One needs silence. The other needs eye contact. Without a shared language, the offers land as distance rather than connection. This is the territory of the Touch Languages™: Juliette's framework for understanding how different bodies need to be approached and met.

Accumulated, unrepaired rupture

Every relationship has ruptures. The variable is what follows. Repair that is partial, or repair that is performed rather than felt, leaves residue. The body holds what was not fully resolved. Given enough accumulation, the body begins to brace before anything has even happened, protecting against the pattern it has learned to expect.

What Changes This

The body that has learned to brace can learn something different. The nervous system is plastic. What was learned can be, with patience and specificity, updated.

It requires new data, not new conversations about the old data. Talking about it in the same dynamic that created it usually deepens the pattern rather than changing it. What changes the pattern is new physical experience, repeated, within a structure the nervous system can trust.

This is why structured touch works where open-ended attempts at more intimacy do not. The structure is the new data. It tells the nervous system: this is different. This is bounded. This is safe to be present for.

It works slowly, then not so slowly. The body updates quickly once it has enough new experience to replace the old prediction. Most couples report a shift within weeks, not because something romantic happened. The nervous system got new information and changed its response accordingly.

"You did not stop touching because you stopped loving. You stopped touching because the body stopped feeling safe enough to stay open. Give the body safety, and it opens again."
