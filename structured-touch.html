Structured Touch: Why structure creates intimacy

The whole industry sells spontaneity. This is why that is wrong, and what works instead.

By Juliette Karaman · Trauma-Informed Intimacy Expert

The prevailing story about intimacy is that it should be natural. Spontaneous. That if you have to plan it, schedule it, structure it, something is already wrong.

This story is doing enormous damage.

It means that when intimacy goes quiet, when the touch stops landing, when the bed becomes a place where you both read and hope the other falls asleep first, people blame themselves. They think the love has gone. They think they are shut down. They think that real intimacy would have just happened, and the fact that it hasn't means something is fundamentally wrong with them.

None of this is true. What is true is that the body only opens inside a container. Spontaneity is not the condition for intimacy. It is the enemy of it, for anyone whose nervous system has ever learned to protect itself.

What Structured Touch Is

Structured touch is touch that has a beginning, a middle, and an end. A time limit. A clear agreement about what is happening and what is not. A defined intention.

This is not clinical. It is not unromantic. It is the opposite: it is what makes romance possible for nervous systems that have been through something.

When touch is open-ended, when there is no boundary around what might happen next, the nervous system stays alert. It cannot afford to relax, because it does not know where this is going. It stays half-braced, half-present, never fully in the moment because it is managing the unknown.

Give that same nervous system a container: fifteen minutes, this is what we are doing, this is where it ends, and something changes. The body can afford to arrive, because it knows the walls. It does not have to monitor the situation. It can be in it.

"I do not fix the relationship. I design the room the body finally trusts enough to come back into."

The Elements of Structured Touch

Time

A defined duration, fifteen minutes is Juliette's most frequently used container, does two things. It makes the practice achievable (no couple argues about fifteen minutes), and it gives the nervous system a known endpoint, which makes full presence inside the container possible.

Agreement

Both people know what is happening. There is no ambiguity about intention. This is not foreplay with an implied destination. This is touch, for its own sake, within this agreed boundary. Agreement is safety. Ambiguity is threat.

Intention

Structured touch has a purpose that is not outcome. The purpose is presence. Noticing. Being with what is, rather than heading toward something. When the body knows it is not being asked to perform or produce, it can simply be.

Anchor

Something to return to when attention drifts, which it will. This is where Touch Base® does its work. A specific sensation, a point of contact, a breath. Somewhere to come back to when the mind goes elsewhere.

Why This Is Not What People Expect

Most couples who come to Juliette's work expect to be given techniques. New moves. Better approaches. What they find instead is structure, and then discover that structure is what they needed.

One client described their first structured touch practice as "the first time in eleven years that I wasn't performing and he wasn't performing and we were just both actually there." They had been doing the motions of intimacy for over a decade. Structured touch gave them the conditions to actually be in it.

The structure is not the point. The structure is what makes the point possible.

Building Your Own Structure

Structured touch does not require a programme or a course to begin. It requires an agreement and a timer. Start here:

  • Agree on fifteen minutes. Set a timer so neither person is monitoring the clock.
  • Agree on what this is not. This practice is not foreplay. It is not leading anywhere. Saying this explicitly removes the ambiguity that keeps the nervous system on alert.
  • Begin with Touch Base®. Each person anchors in their own body before touching another. Read the practice here →
  • Stay with what is there. Not what you think should be there. Not what used to be there. What is actually present, in this moment.
  • When the timer ends, stop. The ending is as important as the beginning. Honour the container.
