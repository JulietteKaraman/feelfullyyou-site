Structured Communication: the Cards and the dyad

One prompt. Fifteen minutes. Three responses only. The conversation that actually lands.

By Juliette Karaman · Creator of the Intimacy and Communication Cards™

Most couples have the same conversation about intimacy over and over. It starts in one place, goes sideways, ends unresolved. Someone defends. Someone withdraws. The pattern named at the beginning is still named at the end, with more hurt attached to it than it started with.

The conversation is not failing because the people in it do not care. It is failing because it has no structure. It is open-ended. It has no agreed format, no defined role for each person, no container that tells the nervous system what is expected of it and when this ends. Without structure, the conversation becomes a test, and the body treats tests as threat.

Structured Communication is Juliette Karaman's method for changing this. It is the methodology behind the Intimacy and Communication Cards™, and it is built on one principle: the speaker needs to be received, not responded to. Those are not the same thing.

The Structure

One prompt

One person draws a card. The card carries a single question or prompt, specific, body-led, designed to open something real rather than something manageable. The speaker answers it. Not from what they think is the right answer. Not from what they imagine their partner wants to hear. From wherever they actually are in this moment.

The speaker's only job

Speak from the body. Answer from sensation and truth, not from performance or protection. This is harder than it sounds for most people. The habit of managing what comes out, editing for the listener's response, giving the version of the truth that feels safest, is deeply ingrained. The structure supports speaking before the edit happens.

The listener's three responses, and only three

This is the element that changes everything. The listener has exactly three available responses:

  • Thank you. Receives what was said. Acknowledges it arrived. Does not evaluate, interpret, or expand.
  • Clarify that. Asks the speaker to say more about one specific thing, not to probe, not to challenge, simply to understand more completely.
  • Summarise that. Reflects back what was heard, in the listener's own words. Checks that what arrived is what was sent.

No fixing. No defending. No advice. No redirecting to the listener's own experience. No "I understand, and actually what I feel is..." No interpretation of why the speaker feels what they feel. Just receiving.

"The body will not open into physical safety until words have somewhere safe to land first. Structured Communication builds that landing place."

Why Only Three Responses

This is the question most people ask when they first encounter the method. Why such a rigid constraint? Why can the listener not simply respond naturally?

Because "naturally" is the pattern. What most people do naturally when their partner shares something vulnerable is: interpret it, add context from their own experience, gently correct what they see as misperception, or move quickly to reassurance or solution. All of these feel helpful from the inside. All of them, from the speaker's side, communicate: what you said is not quite right, let me fix it.

The speaker learns, over years of this, not to say the real thing. They give the manageable version. The version that will not require too much navigation. The real thing, the thing that might actually change something between them, stays unsaid.

Three responses removes this entirely. The listener cannot fix, cannot interpret, cannot redirect. They can only receive. For the speaker, this is often the first time they have experienced being genuinely heard without the hearing immediately becoming about the listener. It lands differently. It lands as being met.

The Fifteen-Minute Container

The dyad runs for fifteen minutes. A timer is set. When it ends, the roles switch, the listener becomes the speaker, a new card is drawn. Each person gets equal time. Neither person's experience is more urgent than the other's.

The time limit does what all structures do: it tells the nervous system the shape of what it is in. Fifteen minutes is achievable. Fifteen minutes has an ending. The body can afford to be fully present inside fifteen minutes in a way it cannot afford inside "however long this takes."

Why Communication Has to Come Before Touch

In the Touch Reset methodology, Structured Communication is Part Two. It comes before Structured Touch Dates, not after. This sequence is not arbitrary.

The body does not open into physical safety until words have somewhere safe to land first. If the conversation between two people is unresolved, if things are being felt but not said, if what is said is being managed rather than received, the body carries that residue into physical contact. The touch feels different. Not wrong, exactly. Just not quite landing. Because underneath the touch is everything that has not been said.

Structured Communication clears that. Not all of it, not in one session. Yet enough that the body's prediction of what contact means begins to change. Enough that reaching toward each other carries less weight of the unspoken. Enough that touch has a chance to land as touch, rather than as the continuation of everything left unresolved.
