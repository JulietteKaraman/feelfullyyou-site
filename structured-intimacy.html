The core principle

The body only opens inside a container it trusts.

Across years of body-based work with couples, women, and men, Juliette Karaman has learned one thing that is true across almost every case of intimacy going quiet: the people in it were never given a structure.

They were given advice. They were given date nights. They were given communication books and therapy and romantic weekends. What they were not given was a container, something with clear edges, a defined time, a known ending, inside which the nervous system could actually stop protecting and start arriving.

The nervous system does not relax into open-ended connection. It cannot afford to. Open-ended means anything could happen. Anything could happen means the body has to stay ready, and a body that is staying ready is a body that cannot fully land in touch, in intimacy, or in another person.

Structure is what makes safety possible. Safety is what makes intimacy possible. This is not a workaround. This is how it actually works.

"I do not fix the relationship. I design the room the body finally trusts enough to come back into."

The Three Structures

Each one does something the others cannot.

Structure One

Structured Touch Dates

Time-contained, non-sexual, nothing required to happen at the end. A defined container in which two people practise being physically present with each other, not performing, not heading anywhere, not trying to fix or escalate. Just arriving, together, inside a structure the nervous system can trust.

Built to deepen week by week. The structure is the point. The intimacy follows because the structure made the safety first.

Structure Two

Structured Communication

One prompt. Fifteen minutes. Three responses only: Thank you. Clarify that. Summarise that. No fixing. No defending. No advice. No redirecting. The speaker speaks from the body. The listener holds without interpretation.

This is the structure of the Intimacy and Communication Cards™, the methodology that turns the conversation that goes in circles into the conversation that actually lands.

Structure Three

The Structured Time Limit

Why spontaneity fails

Spontaneity is not the condition for intimacy. For most nervous systems, it is the enemy of it.

The story told by every romance film, every intimacy course that has come before, every piece of advice given to couples who have lost the thread: just be more present. Let it flow. Stop overthinking it.

This advice works for exactly one type of nervous system, one that has never learned to associate intimacy with threat, pressure, agenda, or disappointment. For everyone else, which is most people, in most long relationships, after enough accumulated tension, "just let it happen" is the instruction that keeps them stuck.

Here is what actually happens when touch is open-ended and undefined: the nervous system stays alert. It has to. It does not know where this is going. It does not know what will be required of it. It does not know when it ends. So it monitors. It manages. It stays half-present, half-ready, never fully in the moment because the moment has no edges.

Give that same nervous system a container, this is what we are doing, for this long, this is where it ends, nothing else is required, and something physiologically different becomes possible. The body can arrive. Because it knows the walls. Because it can see the door. Because the structure told it: you are safe inside this.

Structure is not the opposite of intimacy. Structure is what makes intimacy safe enough to actually happen.
